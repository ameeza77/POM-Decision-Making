4.1

The Nature of Decision Making

Choosing the best course of action from several alternatives.

Decision making is one of the most important roles of a manager. A decision is made when a manager chooses the best course of action from several alternatives. Every decision affects organizational performance, resources, employees, customers, and future direction.

Managers decide to solve problems, take opportunities, reduce risks, improve performance, and respond to changes in the business environment. It is not only about choosing what seems correct — it means understanding the situation, gathering information, considering alternatives, and selecting the most suitable action.

Decision making happens at all levels. Top managers make strategic decisions, middle managers make tactical decisions, and lower-level managers make operational decisions. Good decisions help an organization achieve objectives, use resources wisely, and stay competitive.

The Decision Process at a Glance

  • Identify a problem or opportunity
  • Collect relevant information
  • Develop alternative solutions
  • Evaluate possible outcomes
  • Choose and implement the best option
  • Review the result
Example

A café manager notices customer waiting time rising during lunch. They must decide whether to hire more staff, improve the ordering system, redesign the service counter, or introduce online pre-ordering.

4.2

How Managers Make Decisions

From perfect logic to making the best call under real limits.

Managers do not always decide the same way. Some decisions rest on facts and analysis; others depend on experience, judgment, intuition, or pressure from time and resources.

In an ideal situation, managers make rational decisions — they define the problem clearly, collect complete information, evaluate all alternatives, and choose the best solution. In reality, they often face limited time, incomplete information, and uncertainty. So they use bounded rationality: making the best possible decision within the limits of available information, time, cost, and ability.

Instead of the perfect answer, managers may choose a satisfactory one — this is called satisficing. They may also use intuition: experience, instinct, and quick judgment. Intuition helps when experience is strong or time is short, but it should be supported by facts when possible.

Example

Choosing a new supplier, a manager would ideally compare cost, quality, delivery time, reliability, and reputation. If the order is urgent, they may pick a supplier with acceptable quality and fast delivery — not perfect, but good enough.

4.3

Types of Decisions and Problems

The type of problem shapes the type of decision needed.

A programmed decision handles routine, repetitive problems and usually follows rules, procedures, or policies — for example, approving staff leave under company policy.

A non-programmed decision handles new, unusual, or complex problems. It needs judgment, creativity, and analysis because there is no ready-made solution — for example, responding to a competitor's surprise product.

Problems can also be well-structured (clear and familiar) or poorly structured (unclear, complex, uncertain).

Example

A refund covered by company policy is a programmed decision. A public social-media complaint that may damage the brand's image calls for a non-programmed decision.

4.4

Decision-Making Models

Four lenses for understanding how decisions actually get made.

The rational model assumes managers are logical and can reach the best decision through a systematic process — useful when information is available and the problem is clear.

The administrative model recognizes real limits such as incomplete information, time pressure, and uncertainty. It is linked to bounded rationality and satisficing.

The intuitive model explains decisions made from experience, feelings, and quick judgment — common when speed is needed or experience is strong.

The political model views decision making as shaped by power, negotiation, conflict, and competing interests — common where stakeholders pursue different goals.

Example

A new marketing campaign might be analyzed with the rational model using customer data. But if top managers clash over budget and departmental interests, the political model is also at play.

4.5

Decision-Making Steps

A systematic process reduces confusion and helps justify the choice.

Real decisions rarely follow a perfect sequence, but the steps are a reliable guide. It starts with identifying the problem — the real issue, not just the symptom. Low sales may be a symptom; the real problem might be weak positioning or poor service.

Next, set the decision criteria (cost, quality, time, risk, customer impact), generate alternatives, then evaluate their strengths, costs, benefits, and risks. Select the option that best fits the criteria, implement it properly, and finally evaluate the outcome to see whether the problem was solved.

The 7 Steps

  • Identify the problem
  • Identify decision criteria
  • Generate alternatives
  • Evaluate alternatives
  • Select the best alternative
  • Implement the decision
  • Evaluate the outcome
Example

A retail store with declining sales: the manager identifies the problem, sets criteria (cost, customer response), develops alternatives (promotions, product changes), evaluates them, selects a promotion, implements it, and reviews sales after one month.

Case Study: A Bakery's Delivery Problem

The Situation

A small bakery sells cakes, pastries, and lunch boxes through walk-in customers and online orders. Lately it has received many complaints about late deliveries and incorrect orders, and some customers posted negative comments on social media. Sales dropped 15% in two months.

The owner asked the outlet manager to investigate. She found three issues: orders arrive through several channels with no proper recording system; staff sometimes forget special requests like "less sugar" or "no nuts"; and delivery riders are only booked once an order is ready, causing peak-hour delays.

Alternatives on the table:

  1. Use a simple online form for all online orders
  2. Hire one part-time admin to manage orders
  3. Buy a paid ordering system with auto-receipts and delivery scheduling
  4. Limit online orders during peak hours

The owner wants a solution that is affordable, easy for staff to use, and able to improve customer satisfaction quickly.

Guided Questions — Tap to Reveal

1. Identify the main problem.▸
The main problem is poor order management, which causes late deliveries, incorrect orders, customer complaints, and declining sales.
2. Is this a programmed or non-programmed decision?▸
Mainly non-programmed — the problem is complex, affects several areas, and has no single standard solution. The manager must analyze alternatives and select the best option.
3. What decision criteria should be used?▸
Cost, ease of use, speed of implementation, accuracy of order recording, staff readiness, customer satisfaction, and delivery efficiency.
4. Evaluate two alternatives.▸
An online form is low-cost and easy to implement but may still need manual checking. A paid system is more complete and reduces errors, but costs more and requires staff training.
5. Which alternative would you recommend? Why?▸
Start with an online form plus one assigned staff member to monitor orders — affordable, quick, and able to reduce mistakes. Once sales improve, upgrade to a paid ordering system.
